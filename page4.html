Comparing Phytomass Surface Quotient and Temperature Anomaly: Mapping Global Cities

This project endeavors to evaluate the relationship between phytomass surface quotient and temperature anomalies for global cities. A 5-year global map for temperature anomalies is produced as a raster mean statistic for 60 temperature value maps for the 2013-2018 period. For 14 cities, the total phytomass quotient is estimated in square meters by dividing the sum of the surface areas of phytomass features by the urban area for each city. The relationship between the two values is charted. The chart trendline results indicate a direct relationship between phytomass quotient and temperature anomaly for the selected cities. Such a result suggests that phytomass cover may not reliably mitigate temperature anomaly. Further analysis for mitigation variables beyond the relationship of phytomass and temperature anomaly is warranted.

American Politics: an urban vs. rural spatial analysis

The history of the rural vs. urban divide that exists in American began primarily with unions forming in factories where urban cities where located. Historically unions have primarily supported the Democratic Party and that has grown exponentially as younger people move to cities for jobs or careers. This GIS research project utilizes accessible demographic data for analysis of this 'urban vs rural' split in current American politics.

Mapping Need for Increased Access to Public Transit in Detroit, MI

The public transportation system in Detroit, Michigan consists of sparse, inconsistent bus service and limited, underfunded train and light rail systems. Increased access to an expanded public transit system would be an invaluable resource to the marginalized communities of Detroit that have long suffered in oppressive social, political, and economic systems. I conducted a suitability analysis to map where increased access to transportation in Detroit would be most impactful by evaluating access to the existing system and population demographics, producing a map that highlights geographic pockets of the city where the need for increased transportation is greatest. Areas with the strongest need include communities in the Northwest, Southwest, and Eastern side of the city. Need is the lowest downtown (close to the People Mover and sports stadiums) and along the main arterial avenues that extend diagonally from downtown (Michigan, West Grand River, Woodward, Gratiot, and East Jefferson).

Urban Inequality in Mexico City

In the past, urbanization has been associated with sharp increases in economic and social development. Nevertheless, contemporary urbanization is failing to produce the same kind of benefits for developing countries. More than two-thirds of the worldâ€™s population live in cities that are more unequal today than they were in 1980. In Latin America, some of the main problems of urbanization are the highly unequal distribution of income, access to public services and social development. The results are a dual urban economy and a highly segregated urban social structure. This project aims to map urban inequality in Mexico City, in terms of access to urban services, infrastructure and dimensions of human development (education, access to health care and social security). For this purpose, the Social Development Index is used as a measure of multidimensional poverty. The Index and its components are later analyzed through a regression analysis to determine the variables that have a major effect in the Index and that can determine the level of development for each neighborhood.
